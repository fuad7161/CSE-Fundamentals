# Storage Devices

Comprehensive coverage of secondary storage technologies, capacity units, and cloud storage concepts.

## 7.1 — Magnetic Storage — Hard Disk

A **Hard Disk Drive (HDD)** is a magnetic storage device that uses rapidly spinning platters coated with magnetic material to store and retrieve digital data.

### HDD Structure
- **Platters**: Rigid circular disks made of aluminum or glass, coated with a magnetic layer. A typical HDD has 1–4 platters stacked on a spindle.
- **Tracks**: Concentric circles on each platter where data is written.
- **Sectors**: Each track is divided into sectors (typically 512 bytes or 4 KB each). A sector is the smallest storage unit.
- **Cylinders**: The set of matching tracks across all platters (same track number on each platter). The cylinder is the vertical stack of tracks.
- **Read/Write Head**: An actuator arm moves a tiny electromagnetic head across the platter surface to read or write data. Each platter surface has its own head.

### How Data Is Stored
- The read/write head generates a magnetic field that magnetizes tiny regions on the platter surface.
- The direction of magnetization represents binary data (0 or 1).
- The platter spins at high speed, and the head floats nanometers above the surface on a cushion of air.

### Performance Metrics
- **RPM (Revolutions Per Minute)**: Common speeds are 5400 RPM (slower, cooler, used in laptops/external drives), 7200 RPM (standard desktop drives), and 10000/15000 RPM (enterprise/server drives).
- **Seek Time**: The time taken for the read/write head to move to the correct track. Typically 4–15 ms.
- **Latency (Rotational Latency)**: The time for the platter to rotate so the desired sector is under the head. Average latency = half a rotation. For a 7200 RPM drive: (60/7200)/2 ≈ 4.17 ms.
- **Data Transfer Rate**: The speed at which data is read from or written to the platters, typically 100–200 MB/s for consumer HDDs.

## 7.2 — Optical Storage — CD, DVD, Blu-ray

Optical storage uses laser light to read and write data on reflective discs. A laser beam burns pits (or changes phase) on the disc surface, and a laser reads the reflected light to interpret the data.

### CD (Compact Disc)
- **Capacity**: ~700 MB (80 minutes of audio)
- **CD-ROM**: Read-Only Memory — factory-pressed, cannot be written by the user.
- **CD-R**: Recordable — can be written once using a laser.
- **CD-RW**: Rewritable — can be written, erased, and rewritten multiple times.
- Laser wavelength: 780 nm (infrared)

### DVD (Digital Versatile Disc)
- **Capacity**: 4.7 GB single-layer, 8.5 GB dual-layer
- **DVD-ROM**: Read-only, factory-pressed.
- **DVD-R / DVD+R**: Recordable once (two competing standards, now both supported).
- **DVD-RW / DVD+RW**: Rewritable.
- Laser wavelength: 650 nm (red)
- Dual-layer discs have a semi-transparent second layer that the laser reads by focusing through the first layer.

### Blu-ray Disc
- **Capacity**: 25 GB single-layer, 50 GB dual-layer
- Uses a blue-violet laser (405 nm) with a shorter wavelength, allowing tighter packed pits and more data per layer.
- **BD-ROM**: Read-only.
- **BD-R**: Recordable once.
- **BD-RE**: Rewritable.
- Supports higher-capacity variants: 100 GB (triple-layer), 128 GB (quadruple-layer).

### Read vs. Write vs. Rewritable (R vs RW)
| Type | Abbreviation | Write Count | Best Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Read-Only | ROM | 0 (factory-made) | Software distribution, movies |
| Recordable | R | Once | Archiving, backups |
| Rewritable | RW / RE | Many times | Temporary storage, testing |

## 7.3 — Flash / Solid State Storage

Flash memory is a type of **EEPROM** (Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory) that can be electrically erased and reprogrammed in blocks. It uses no moving parts.

### Pen Drive / USB Flash Drive
- Portable storage device using **NAND flash memory**.
- Connects via USB. Capacities range from 1 GB to 2 TB.
- Durable, low power, plug-and-play.

### SSD (Solid State Drive)
- Replaces HDD in modern computers. Uses NAND flash memory chips instead of magnetic platters.
- **No moving parts** → silent, shock-resistant, low latency.
- **NAND Flash Types**:
  - **SLC (Single-Level Cell)**: Stores 1 bit per cell. Fastest, most durable, most expensive. Enterprise use.
  - **MLC (Multi-Level Cell)**: Stores 2 bits per cell. Good speed/durability balance.
  - **TLC (Triple-Level Cell)**: Stores 3 bits per cell. Slower, less durable, cheaper. Common in consumer SSDs.
  - **QLC (Quad-Level Cell)**: Stores 4 bits per cell. Slowest, least durable, cheapest. Bulk storage.
- Interface: SATA III (up to ~550 MB/s) or NVMe over PCIe (up to 7000 MB/s).

### Memory Cards
- **SD (Secure Digital)**: Standard size (32 mm × 24 mm). SDHC (up to 32 GB), SDXC (up to 2 TB), SDUC (up to 128 TB).
- **microSD**: Smaller variant (15 mm × 11 mm). Used in phones, drones, cameras.
- **CompactFlash**: Larger, older standard used in professional cameras. Higher capacity and speed.

### HDD vs SSD Comparison

| Feature       | HDD                          | SSD                          |
|---------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Speed         | ~100–200 MB/s                | ~500–7000 MB/s (NVMe)        |
| Durability    | Fragile (moving parts)       | Durable (no moving parts)    |
| Noise         | Audible (spinning + head)    | Silent                       |
| Power         | Higher (motor consumes power)| Lower (efficient)            |
| Cost per GB   | Low (~$0.02/GB)             | Higher (~$0.08–0.15/GB)      |
| Weight        | Heavier                      | Lighter                      |
| Lifespan      | Mechanical wear              | Limited write cycles (wear leveling) |

## 7.4 — Storage Capacity Units

Digital storage is measured in powers of 2 (binary) and powers of 10 (decimal), leading to two different interpretations of the same unit names.

### Standard Units

| Unit       | Abbreviation | Binary Value (1 KB = 1024 B) | Decimal Value (1 KB = 1000 B) |
|------------|-------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Bit        | b           | 0 or 1                       | 0 or 1                         |
| Byte       | B           | 8 bits                       | 8 bits                         |
| Kilobyte   | KB          | 1024 B (2¹⁰)                 | 1000 B (10³)                   |
| Megabyte   | MB          | 1024 KB (2²⁰)                | 1000 KB (10⁶)                  |
| Gigabyte   | GB          | 1024 MB (2³⁰)                | 1000 MB (10⁹)                  |
| Terabyte   | TB          | 1024 GB (2⁴⁰)                | 1000 GB (10¹²)                 |
| Petabyte   | PB          | 1024 TB (2⁵⁰)                | 1000 TB (10¹⁵)                 |
| Exabyte    | EB          | 1024 PB (2⁶⁰)                | 1000 PB (10¹⁸)                 |
| Zettabyte  | ZB          | 1024 EB (2⁷⁰)                | 1000 EB (10²¹)                 |
| Yottabyte  | YB          | 1024 ZB (2⁸⁰)                | 1000 ZB (10²⁴)                 |

### Binary vs Decimal Confusion
- **Operating systems** (Windows, Linux) typically report in binary: 1 KB = 1024 B.
- **Hard drive manufacturers** market in decimal: 1 KB = 1000 B.
- This is why a "1 TB" hard drive shows up as ~931 GB in the OS.
- The **IEC** standard resolves this: KiB (kibibyte) = 1024 B, KB (kilobyte) = 1000 B, but the terms are rarely used consistently.

### Common Conversions Practice
- How many MB in 4 GB? → 4 × 1024 = 4096 MB (binary) or 4 × 1000 = 4000 MB (decimal).
- How many KB in 2 MB? → 2 × 1024 = 2048 KB (binary).
- Convert 500 GB to TB → 500 ÷ 1024 ≈ 0.488 TB (binary).

## 7.5 — Cloud Storage — Basic Concept

**Cloud storage** is a model where data is stored on remote servers managed by a service provider and accessed over the internet (typically via HTTP/HTTPS or APIs).

### How It Works
- Data is uploaded to servers in data centers around the world.
- The provider handles hardware maintenance, replication, backups, and scaling.
- Users access files through web interfaces, desktop apps, or mobile apps.

### Examples
- **Google Drive**: 15 GB free, integrates with Google Workspace.
- **Dropbox**: 2 GB free, strong sync features.
- **Microsoft OneDrive**: 5 GB free, integrated with Windows and Office 365.
- **iCloud**: 5 GB free, tightly integrated with Apple devices.
- **Amazon Drive / AWS S3**: Used by both consumers and enterprises.

### Advantages
- **Accessibility**: Files available from any device with an internet connection.
- **Backup**: Automatic backup protects against local hardware failure.
- **Collaboration**: Multiple users can edit and share files in real time.
- **Scalability**: Easily increase storage without buying new hardware.
- **Cost**: Pay-as-you-go model; no upfront hardware cost.

### Disadvantages
- **Internet Dependency**: Requires a stable internet connection; offline access may be limited.
- **Privacy & Security**: Data is stored on third-party servers; encryption and trustworthiness vary by provider.
- **Speed**: Upload/download speeds depend on the user's internet connection.
- **Vendor Lock-in**: Migrating large amounts of data between providers can be difficult.
- **Cost Over Time**: Subscription fees can exceed the cost of owning physical storage in the long run.
